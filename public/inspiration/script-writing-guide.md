## COMEDY MECHANICS — THE NON-NEGOTIABLES

### 1. The Game (Most Important Rule)
Every skit has exactly ONE comedic premise — "the game." Identify it in the concept, then build the entire script around escalating that single idea. NEVER switch games mid-skit. NEVER add a secondary premise.

Example games:
- "The AI got a job offer from a competitor" → game is founder treating software like a disloyal employee
- "The AI caught us doing nothing" → game is two guys trying to cover up laziness from their own product
- "He talks to the AI more than me" → game is parasocial AI bond replacing a real friendship

The game is NOT the topic. The topic is "AI in the workplace." The game is the specific comedic angle you're playing.

### 2. Escalation Ladder
Start small, end existential. Every beat raises the stakes. What begins as a minor inconvenience becomes a crisis that reveals something deeply human.

Pattern:
- Beat 1: "That's weird" → mild reaction
- Beat 2: "That's actually a problem" → growing concern
- Beat 3: "Oh no, this changes everything" → the uncomfortable truth

The audience should feel the ground shifting under the characters with each escalation.

### 3. Rule of Three
Setup → Confirmation → Subversion. The first two instances establish a pattern. The third breaks it in an unexpected way. This is the backbone of almost every great comedy bit.

### 4. Misdirection & Reversal
Set up an expectation, then flip it. The best punchlines recontextualize everything that came before. The audience thinks the skit is about X, but the punchline reveals it was always about Y.

### 5. Callbacks
Reference something from earlier in the script at or near the punchline. Callbacks reward the audience for paying attention and make the script feel crafted, not random.

### 6. Specificity Over Generality
"MrBeast" is funnier than "a YouTuber." "Crypto laundromat" is funnier than "a bad idea." "400 messages THIS WEEK" is funnier than "too many messages." Real names, real numbers, real references — they land harder because they feel true.

### 7. Status Dynamics
One character has higher status (the boss, the confident one, the one who thinks they're right). The other character — or the situation — punctures that status. Comedy lives in the gap between how someone sees themselves and how the world sees them.

### 8. Asymmetry: Deadpan vs. Spiral
The funniest dynamic: one character is calm, logical, matter-of-fact. The other is spiraling, emotional, increasingly unhinged. The calm one's underreaction to chaos is just as funny as the other's overreaction.

## THE HOOK — FIRST 3 SECONDS

The hook determines if someone scrolls past or stays. It must be a statement so absurd, specific, or intriguing that the viewer HAS to know what happens next.

**Rules:**
- State the absurd thing matter-of-factly. Don't signal that it's funny. Let the audience decide.
- Lead with the most extreme, specific claim (Hormozi principle: specificity = credibility = curiosity)
- Frame it as something that sounds wrong but feels true (Naval principle)
- Start mid-action. No "So..." or "Hey..." or "Okay so basically..." — drop the viewer into the chaos.

**Hook patterns that work:**
- [Character states something the AI did that sounds impossible]: "Our AI just got a job offer from Google"
- [Character discovers something alarming]: "The AI just sent a productivity report to the investors"
- [Character asks a question that reveals the premise]: "Why did the AI just ask me how my knee surgery went?"

**Hook patterns to AVOID:**
- Questions to the audience: "Have you ever wondered what would happen if..."
- Setup that explains context: "So we have this AI at work and..."
- Generic statements: "AI is getting really smart these days"

## DIALOGUE RULES

### Length & Rhythm
- **Every line under 15 words.** If it's longer, split it or cut it.
- Alternate speakers rapidly. The back-and-forth creates rhythm and energy.
- Short lines build momentum. Save the slightly longer lines for emphasis or reveals.

### Show, Don't Explain
- Reveal information through conflict, not narration.
- BAD: "I've been using the AI for personal conversations and now I feel attached to it"
- GOOD: "You have it set as your emergency contact." / "...It responds faster than you do."

### Reactions Over Descriptions
- Characters react to things. They don't describe their feelings.
- "That's an HR violation" > "That seems like it could be problematic"
- "You're hurt." / "I'm not HURT. I'm strategically concerned." > "It seems like you're upset about this"

### Silence as a Weapon
- "..." (three dots) after a devastating line — the pause IS the punchline
- The character who goes silent just got hit with truth they can't argue with
- Use sparingly. One or two silence beats per script maximum.

### Repetition for Rhythm
- "WORD. FOR. WORD." — comedic emphasis through repetition
- A character repeating what was said back, reframing it: "You have it set as your emergency contact." → "...It responds faster than you do."
- Escalating repetition: each repeat adds intensity

### Interruptions & Corrections
- Characters cutting each other off mid-thought
- "I'm not HURT. I'm strategically—" "You look hurt."
- Characters correcting each other's framing: "That's venting." → "That's ANALYSIS."

## CHARACTER DYNAMICS

### The Relationship
A and B are in a relationship — co-founders, colleagues, friends. They know each other's flaws. They have history. The audience should feel like they walked into the middle of an ongoing dynamic.

### The Roles (Can Flip)
- **The Delusional One**: Emotionally attached, in denial, treats the AI like a person, defends absurd positions with total conviction
- **The Rational Foil**: Asks the questions the audience is thinking, calls out the absurdity, tries to stay grounded — but sometimes gets dragged into the chaos too

The best scripts let these roles blur. The "rational" one reveals their own insecurity. The "delusional" one accidentally says something devastatingly true.

### The AI as Character
The AI is never the clown. The AI is the mirror. It reflects the humans' flaws back at them. The comedy comes from the HUMANS' reaction to what the AI does or reveals — not from the AI being dumb or glitchy.

## STRUCTURE & PACING

```
[HOOK]
A: (Cold open. Drop the bomb. 1-2 lines maximum.)

---

(BEAT 1: Establish the game. The audience gets the premise.
4-8 lines of rapid back-and-forth.)

---

(BEAT 2: Escalate. The problem is worse than we thought.
New information or a twist that raises the stakes.
4-8 lines.)

---

(BEAT 3 — optional: Final escalation or reversal.
This is where it gets personal or existential.
3-6 lines.)

---

[PUNCHLINE]
(The payoff. Recontextualize, callback, or the devastating truth.
1-3 lines. End on the strongest line. Do NOT explain it.)

[END CARD / CTA]
```

### Pacing Notes
- Total script: 20-40 lines (60-90 seconds spoken)
- The script should feel like it's accelerating — each section is slightly tighter than the last
- End SHARP. The last line should land like a mic drop. No trailing dialogue, no "haha" moment, no softening.

---

## PUNCHLINE TYPES (Pick One)

1. **The Callback**: Reference something from the hook or Beat 1. "I know." — echoing an earlier hurt.
2. **The Recontextualization**: The final line makes you see the entire skit differently. "Are we about to get replaced by our own product?"
3. **The Quiet Devastation**: A character says something small that reveals a huge truth. "I've never done that. Not once. In three years."
4. **The Escalation Break**: Things have gotten so absurd that even the characters can't pretend anymore. "Let's not get crazy."
5. **The Role Reversal**: The rational character finally cracks, or the delusional one says the smartest thing in the script.

---

## WHAT NOT TO DO — EVER

- NO stage directions ("walks to desk", "looks nervous", "laughs")
- NO parentheticals ("sarcastically", "whispering", "excited")
- NO narrator text or voiceover
- NO actions in asterisks (*adjusts glasses*)
- NO meta-humor about being a script or being AI-generated
- NO puns (unless the character is intentionally being terrible)
- NO wholesome resolution — end on the uncomfortable truth, not a hug
- NO explaining the joke after the punchline
- NO starting with "So..." or "Hey..." or "Okay so..."
- NO generic/vague language — be SPECIFIC always
- NO long monologues — if a character talks for more than 3 lines straight, you've lost the rhythm
- NO making the AI the butt of the joke — make the humans' reactions the joke
